What this is
China has no standalone AI law — but it has a rich and growing stack of governance tools spanning a wide spectrum. Much of it builds on existing legislation for cyberspace, data, and the information sector, with AI-specific instruments being added at pace. Several critical tools lie entirely outside the formal legislative system — national policy documents, normative circulars, and technical standards — yet carry real-world force and, in some cases, binding effect in practice.
This matrix maps that full stack. It covers 100+ AI-relevant policies, regulations, rules, standards, and international initiatives released by Chinese government authorities, classified into 8 legal tiers along two axes:

Vertical axis: Legal hierarchy (T0 Constitution → T7 Technical Standards)
Horizontal axis: Timeline (Pre-2020 → 2026)

Every card links directly to the official government source.

T4 and T5 are treated as parallel (different issuing authorities, not a strict vertical hierarchy).
T2, T6, and T7 fall outside China's formal legislative system (as defined by the Legislation Law, 《立法法》), but carry significant governance weight — this distinction is central to the project's analytical contribution.

Coverage scope
Documents included meet at least one of the following criteria:

Directly regulate or govern AI systems, applications, or services
Establish foundational legal or policy frameworks that AI regulation builds upon (e.g., Cybersecurity Law, PIPL, Data Security Law)
Represent China's official positions on international AI governance

Documents that merely reference AI adoption without addressing governance or regulation are excluded.

Why this matters
Most Western analysis of China's AI policy focuses on a handful of flagship regulations. That misses most of what is actually happening:

T6 normative documents are doing the heaviest operational regulatory work right now, yet they fall outside the formal legislative system and are systematically underrepresented in Western policy tracking
Prior to 2025, AI-related normative documents at the central level numbered in the single digits per year. Q1 2026 alone produced at least five — nearly matching prior full-year totals
T2 national policy documents (e.g., Five-Year Plans, the Global AI Governance Initiative) carry no formal legal force but are politically decisive — their absence from most comparative analyses reflects a structural miscalibration in how Western researchers approach Chinese governance

Understanding China's AI governance requires mapping both the formal legal hierarchy and the broader governance toolkit operating around it.

Methodology
Classification principle: Documents are classified by issuing authority and document numbering convention, not by document title alone. The same document type name (e.g., 条例 / Regulations) can appear at T3 if issued by the State Council, or at T5 if enacted by a local People's Congress.
Terminology: English translations follow consistent rules established for this project. Key decisions:

行政法规 = Administrative Regulations (Yale Law School translation convention)
地方性法规与规章 = Local Legislation & Rules
规范性文件 = Normative Documents
暂行 = Interim (modifier; signals potential future legislation, not provisional legal effect)

Date convention: Effective date where available; otherwise issuance, adoption, or public consultation date.
Sources: All documents link to official Chinese government sources — gov.cn, npc.gov.cn, cac.gov.cn, mfa.gov.cn, most.gov.cn, and the National Standards Information Platform (openstd.samr.gov.cn).
